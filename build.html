Step 2Electronics & code

Two small boards do the work: a camera unit that photographs the pod and sends the image to the cloud, and the AlgaeScope — a spectrometer that measures the algae's colour across eight wavelengths.

Camera unit: a Seeed Studio XIAO ESP32S3 Sense. It uploads a photo to a free Adafruit IO account every few seconds, which is what appears live on the map. Full setup — including the Adafruit steps and the common pitfalls — is in the GitHub README.

AlgaeScope (spectrometer): a XIAO ESP32S3 with an AS7341 spectral sensor, an OLED display, and a white LED light source. It hosts its own dashboard on your WiFi (see Step 4).

Step 3Inoculation of microalgae

The pod uses Chlamydomonas reinhardtii, a freshwater green alga, as its living sensor. You inoculate a starter culture into your river-water sample and let it grow.

Culture & conditions

  • Organism: Chlamydomonas reinhardtii
  • Light: continuous, roughly 40–150 µE m⁻² s⁻¹
  • Temperature: environmental (ideal around 25 °C)
  • Organism-to-media ratio: 1:5

You can order the Chlamydomonas reinhardtii culture from Blades Biological.

Protocol

  1. Add 50 ml of a river-water sample from your chosen location.
  2. Add 10 ml of Chlamydomonas reinhardtii to the pod.
  3. Mix the culture thoroughly.
  4. Insert the air-pump tube into the pod and close it with the air-filter enclosure provided.
  5. Turn on the air pump for thorough gas exchange, and close the shell for uniform light dispersion.
Step 4The AlgaeScope app

The AlgaeScope doesn't need a separate app to install — the device is the app. When it powers on it connects to your WiFi and runs a small dashboard you open in a web browser.

  1. Flash the AlgaeScope code to the board and open the Arduino Serial Monitor.
  2. It connects to your WiFi and is reachable at http://algaescope.local from any browser on the same network. (If your network or device doesn't support .local addresses, use the numeric IP it prints to the Serial Monitor instead.)
  3. Open that address and the dashboard appears: live spectral readings, absorbance, the stress ratio, a growth curve, grow-light control, and CSV export.

Because it runs on your local network, you view it on the same WiFi as the device — there's no public server or login. Readings are held in the device's memory while it's running.
